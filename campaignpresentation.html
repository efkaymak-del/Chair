To begin

This is a conversation, not a contest.

Since I'm the only candidate, I'll spare us both the campaign speech. We know each other well. So let me use this time for something more useful — to set out, plainly, how I think we can run a tighter, fairer, and more rewarding department, and to invite your thinking on it.

My single idea is simple: use our heads, and the new tools available to us, to expand what eight people can actually achieve — so that more of our time goes where it belongs: to our students and our scholarship.

Pillar One · Administrative capacity

Now imagine it filling itself.

  • Activities — a conference, a publication, a workshop — recorded once, at the moment they happen.
  • A meeting's discussion turned, by tools we already own, into formal council decisions and a STAR-ready activity record — drafted automatically, approved by us.
  • The same pool feeds STAR's evidence file, the Rector's Faaliyet score, and YÖK — no pre-visit scramble, ever.

This is exactly what Council Decision 9 already asks of us — a continuous-improvement evidence file. I simply want to make it effortless.

A word on the tools

AI that augments us — never replaces our judgment.

Let me be clear about what I mean and don't mean. These tools remove drudgery; they do not make our decisions. Everything they produce is a draft we approve — minutes, reports, records all come to the Council exactly as our process requires.

The machine does the assembling. We keep the judgment. That is the whole principle — on the administrative side, and, just as importantly, in our teaching.

Pillar Two · Teaching capacity

The same idea makes our teaching go further.

A department our size cannot offer small-group seminars to thirty-eight first-years. But well-designed, AI-assisted simulations can give every student an individual, experiential encounter with difficult theory — at scale, without diluting it.

I build these already. Students don't read about a theory and recite it back — they enact it, are scaffolded through its layers, and are pushed to question its limits.

Pillar Two · Teaching capacity

Theory, lived — then interrogated.

In the Dworkin Auction, students bid for social goods, face Dworkin's own envy test, are struck by randomized brute luck, and must reason through compensation — discovering the theory's tensions from the inside. It ends by mirroring their own values back and feeding their written brief.

Pillar Two · Teaching capacity

Linking learning to practice — for every student.

  • Experiential. Students do the theory, not just hear it.
  • Scaffolded. Built up in stages toward each student's own potential.
  • Critical. Designed to expose where a framework holds and where it breaks.
  • Assessed. The experience flows straight into their written work.

This is how I'd like us to think about teaching together — and how AI lets a small faculty teach as if it were a larger one.

Pillar Three · An interdisciplinary horizon

Sharing resources across departments.

I intend to pursue an interdisciplinary path — so we can share teaching, courses, and strength with our colleagues across the Faculty of Business and Economics. Our International Political Economy teaching is the natural bridge: economics, political science, and IR already meet there.

This one is an invitation, not a promise. It needs the partnership of the Dean and of other departments — and I will work to build it.

Shared programmes make every participating department stronger, and harder to overlook.

How we'll work together

Fairly, and in the open.

Every duty owned and visible; loads shared honestly; the quiet work that usually goes uncounted, seen and credited. Transparency is my standard — and it binds me first.

[Acknowledge here, by name, the colleagues who currently carry more than their share — and say plainly that lightening that load is the first thing this is all for.]

In closing

A tight ship — so we're free to do the work we came here to do.

Less friction. Fairer loads. Better teaching. A department that is seen, well-run, and impossible to overlook — leaving us more time to teach, to mentor, and to think.

Thank you. I'd welcome your thoughts.
